Overview

What is Kosovo?

Kosovo location on the map (highlighted in red)

In 1998 Kosovo was a province in the southern part of Serbia. The genocide, or “ethnic cleansing” as it was called at the time, occured in Kosovo from 1998 to 1999.

Famous Article from Time Magazine



Prejudice / Hatred

Battle of Kosovo


A painting depicting the battle

The tension between two ethnic groups, the Serbs and Muslim Albanians, stems all the way back from the 1389 Battle of Kosovo. The battle took place in Kosovo and was fought between an army led by Serbian Prince Lazar Hrebeljanović defending from the invading army of the Ottoman empire. The Serbs lost the battle and Lazar died fighting, but Lazar was venerated as a saint and the day of this battle is an important part of Serb national and ethnic identity. This is where the underlying hatred Serbians have for invaders and Muslim Albanians comes from.

Serbian Prince Lazar Hrebeljanović


Tension Leading to the Conflict


Slobodan Milošević addresses a crowd

Ethnic Albanians control Kosovo and were the third largest ethnic group at the time of the conflict. Yugoslavia defines Albanians as a “nationality”, not an autonomous nation within, and yet Serbs, Croats, Bosnians, Slovenes, and Macedonians are not classified this way. Serbia viewed Kosovo’s autonomy as threat to Serbian sovereignty and its interests, worrying that it diluted their power. The poverty occuring in Kosovo does not help to ease tensions, either. Since Kosovo was largely inhabited by Muslim Albanians, Serbs and other minorities felt persecuted by the Albanian majority. The Serbs were sometimes subject to harassment or discrimination. When Slobodan Milosevic became president of the Serbian republic in 1989, he placed Kosovo under the control of the Serbian capital. Milosevic and the Serbian minority in Kosovo had long objected to the fact that Muslim Albanians controlled a land they considered sacred. Needless to say, Kosovar Albanians are not happy about their loss of autonomy and resist Serbian control.



Victims / Perpetrators / Methods

The KLA initiated the conflict and violence, and the Serbian forces perpetuated and accelerated the violence during the Kosovo conflict. Atrocities and war crimes were committed on both sides. Not even civilians were safe from these atrocities.



KLA


The Kosovo Liberation Army(KLA) is created in 1996, and begins attacks on Serbian authorities, which includes police and politicians. The violence gradually escalated, and by 1998, the KLA’s attacks could be considered a substantial armed uprising. One example of a particularly brutal incident occurred in September 1998: KLA forces murdered thirty-four people near Glodjane. The local commanders must have ordered it or at the very least known about the mass murder.

KLA Flag



Yugoslav Army / Serbian Police


Bosnian Muslims wait at a checkpoint


Ethnic Albanian crying in front of her burning home

Yugoslav and Serbian forces responded to the KLA’s attacks with a ruthless counteroffensive and a program of what they called “ethnic cleansing” - essentially, genocide. Serbian forces drove out all of Kosovo’s ethnic Albanians, displacing hundreds of thousands of people. In 1998 alone the Serbian military and police conflict with Kosovar Albanians killed 1,500 Albanians and displaced 400,000. During their campaign of ethnic cleansing, the army and police surrounded a village, then shelled it from a distance. Horror then manifested in the form of mass rapes, beatings, tortures, mutilizations, and killings, all meant to force victims from their homes. The remaining villagers were gathered in one place while the police stole personal possessions from them then looted and burned the remains of the village. The men were separated from women and interrogated about the KLA - those suspected of ties with the KLA were executed. Women, children, and the elderly were often expelled. The Serbs then claimed the “cleansed” area as their own. Thousands of Kosovar Albanians fled from their homes. The event that finally caused the international community to take action occurred on January 15, 1999. Serb-allied forces murdered 45 Albanian civilians at Racak, mutilated most of the bodies, then attempted to cover of the horrific event. Widespread media coverage of the massacre enraged the international community.



NATO Intervention


Rambouillet Conference


NATO bombing Serbian targets

The event at Racak finally convinced the US, UK, and other North Atlantic Treaty Organization(NATO) nations to meet Rambouillet, France and discuss how to end the conflict. Negotiations with Milosevic failed to work, so NATO begins a series air strikes on Serbian military targets on March 23, 1999. This operation was known as Operation Allied Force. Unfortunately, Allied Force actually emboldened Milosevic’s forces - they committed even more atrocities of mass rape, beatings and killings. Ethnic cleansing skyrocketed as Milosevic intensified his efforts in clearing Kosovo of its Muslim non-Serb population; hundreds of thousands more ethnic Albanians were driven out of their homes and into neighbouring countries. NATO intensified the air effort, and even considered deploying ground troops to quell the violence, but never did. The air campaign lasted a total of eleven weeks before Milosevic finally accepted a peace agreement that would end the war and agreed to pull Yugoslav troops out of Kosovo.



Ending / Aftermath


An estimated total of 10,000 Kosovar Albanians died between March and June 1999, and about 1.5 million were displaced from their homes(about 90% of the Albanians living in Kosovo at the time). Some 1,500 Serb civilians were killed by NATO’s bombing campaign. The war and NATO’s bombing had left Kosovo in ruins. On the bright side, once Milosevic removed his troops a large peacekeeping force (under NATO command but sanctioned by the UN) moved into Kosovo to restore the war-torn landscape and help Kosovar Albanians return to their homes. By November 1999, over 800,000 were able to move back into the region.

Milosevic’s Fall


Milosevic’s trial

Following the war, large demonstrations for the removal of Milosevic from power began in Serbia. The Kosovo War is the main factor that drove him from power in 2000. Milosevic was soon indicted for his war crimes. He was turned over to the International Criminal Tribunal for the Former Yugoslavia (ICTY) in 2001. However, Milosevic died in his prison cell March 2006, before his trial could be concluded.


Controversy


Critics of NATO’s bombing campaign were quick to point out how NATO was willing to intervene in a European genocide, but had done nothing during the Rwandan genocide of 1994. They also pointed out how the airstrikes had harmed/killed civilians, including the accidental bombing of the Chinese embassy in Belgrade. Supporters of the bombing argue that NATO’s actions were justified because they finally ended the ethnic cleansing in Kosovo.
